LLM Scaling Laws

This note covers the scaling laws for large language models, looking at how cross entropy loss behaves as model size, dataset size, and compute scale up during training. It essentially analyzes how model performance depends on these scale factors versus the model architecture itself. Basically, scale is what drives performance, while the specific shape of the model matters very little.

The key findings for Transformer language models are:

Performance depends strongly on scale and weakly on model shape: Model performance depends on scale, which is driven by three factors: the number of model parameters $N$ (excluding embeddings), size of dataset $D$, and the amount of compute $C$ used for training. Factors and architectural hyperparameters such as depth and width affect the model very weakly.

Smooth power laws: Performance has a power law relationship with the factors mentioned ($N$, $D$, $C$) when not bottlenecked by the other two, with trends spanning more than six orders of magnitude.

Universality of overfitting: Performance improves predictably as long as we scale up $N$ and $D$ in tandem, but things start going downhill if either $N$ or $D$ is held fixed while the other increases. This performance penalty depends predictably on the ratio $\frac{N^{0.74}}{D}$, meaning that every time we increase the model size 8x, we only need to increase the data by roughly 5x.

Universality of training: Training curves follow predictable power laws whose parameters are roughly independent of the model size.

So, if you want better performance, the move is to focus on scaling up the parameters, data, and compute in the right ratios rather than wasting time tuning hyperparameter details like width or depth. Just keep an eye on that overfitting ratio to make sure you do not bottleneck the training. Next up, it is worth looking at how newer scaling laws (like Chinchilla) tweaked these ratios to make training even more compute optimal.
