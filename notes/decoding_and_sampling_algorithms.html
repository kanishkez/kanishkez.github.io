Decoding and Sampling Algorithms

When generating text from a language model, the goal of decoding is to find the most likely or appropriate sequence of tokens given a prompt. Because enumerating all possible sequences is computationally impossible, we rely on various decoding and sampling algorithms to make this task manageable. Let's look at how these different methods work, starting from basic greedy search and moving to more advanced sampling strategies.

Greedy Decoding

At each step, pick the token with the highest probability:

$$x_t = \arg\max_{x_t} P(x_t \mid x_{1:t-1})$$

Example:

  • Prompt: "Once upon a"
  • Model: {"time": 0.7, "dream": 0.2, "moon": 0.1}
  • Choose: "time"

The problem: As you can see, the word "fox" has the highest probability but it's hiding behind a lower probability word "red". This algorithm can't identify words like that and will simply take the token with the highest probability in that instant.

Beam Search

Keep track of the top k partial beams based on cumulative log probability.

  1. Start with the prompt.
  2. Expand each beam with all possible next tokens.
  3. Keep the top k by total log probability.
  4. Repeat until all beams hit the end of sequence token.

It takes in beams and then chooses a beam depending on their total probability.

The problem: Results are more fluent compared to greedy search, but the output often includes repetitions of the same word sequences.

Sampling

Basically, what we do in sampling is we take a subset distribution of tokens and then choose a word in that sample.

Top K Sampling

The K most likely words are filtered and then the next predicted word will be chosen from these words only.

The produced text is often more human sounding than the text produced with the other methods.

One concern though with Top K sampling is that it does not dynamically adapt the number of words that are filtered from the next word probability distribution, as K is fixed. As a consequence, very unlikely words may be selected among these K words if the next word probability distribution is very sharp.

Top p (Nucleus) Sampling

Top p sampling (or nucleus sampling) chooses from the smallest possible set of words whose cumulative probability exceeds the probability p. This way, the number of words in the set can dynamically increase and decrease according to the next word probability distribution.

While Top p sampling seems more elegant than Top K sampling, both methods work well in practice.

Instead of a fixed K, it dynamically chooses the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability $\ge p$ (e.g., 0.9).

Temperature Sampling

The temperature is used to scale the logits (pre softmax activations):

$$\text{Scaled logits} = \frac{\text{Original logits}}{T}$$

After scaling, they are passed to the softmax function.

Here is what happens when you adjust the temperature:

  1. High Temperature ($T > 1$): The scaling effectively "flattens" the distribution, making less probable tokens more likely to be sampled. This results in more "random" or "creative" outputs.
  2. Low Temperature ($0 < T < 1$): The distribution becomes "sharper," concentrating the probability mass on the most likely tokens. This results in more "deterministic" outputs, where the language model is more likely to pick the most probable word according to its training data.
  3. $T = 1$: The logits remain unchanged, and the original probabilities are used for sampling.

Penalties

Some penalties are also added to enforce certain conditions on the next token.

Repetition Penalty

Reduce the probability of tokens that have already been generated.

$$p'(x_t) = \frac{p(x_t)}{(\text{repetition count})^\alpha}$$

Length Penalty

In beam search or scoring:

$$\text{score}(x_{1:t}) = \frac{1}{t^\alpha} \sum_i \log P(x_i \mid x_{1:i-1})$$

Typical Sampling

Every token has a self information value (also called surprisal):

$$I(x_t) = -\log p(x_t)$$

This measures how surprising that token is:

  • A very probable token $\rightarrow$ low surprisal (e.g., "the").
  • A rare token $\rightarrow$ high surprisal (e.g., "xenolith").

Now, the expected surprisal over the whole distribution is the entropy:

$$H(p_t) = \mathbb{E}_{x_t \sim p_t}[-\log p(x_t)] = -\sum_i p(x_i) \log p(x_i)$$

Entropy represents how uncertain the model is about the next token.

A typical token is one whose surprisal $I(x_t)$ is close to the expected entropy $H(p_t)$.

Typical sampling states that we should only sample tokens whose information content is near the average, meaning they are neither too predictable nor too unlikely.

This eliminates:

  • Too predictable $\rightarrow$ repetitive text like "the the the"
  • Too unlikely $\rightarrow$ incoherent words like "dinosaur quantum blizzard"

Typical sampling keeps tokens where the absolute difference between surprisal and entropy is below a threshold $\tau$:

$$\left| -\log p(x_t) - H(p_t) \right| \le \tau$$

The threshold $\tau$ controls diversity:

  • Small $\tau$: strict typicality $\rightarrow$ deterministic, coherent
  • Large $\tau$: allows more randomness

After filtering, the probabilities are renormalized and one token is sampled from that subset.

Typical Sampling Algorithm Steps

Given model logits $z_t$:

  1. Compute probabilities: $$p_t = \text{softmax}(z_t)$$
  2. Compute self information for each token: $$I_i = -\log(p_t[i])$$
  3. Compute entropy: $$H = \sum_i p_t[i] \, I_{i}$$
  4. Compute the difference from entropy: $$d_i = |I_i - H|$$
  5. Sort tokens by $d_i$ (how typical they are).
  6. Keep the smallest subset of tokens such that their cumulative probability $\ge \text{typical } p$ (analogous to top p, often 0.9 to 0.95).
  7. Renormalize and sample.

Modern large language models like ChatGPT and Claude typically combine these techniques, using temperature along with top p or typical sampling depending on the task. In practice, finding the right balance between coherence and creativity comes down to tweaking these settings for the specific application.
